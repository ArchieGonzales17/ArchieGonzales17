Dataset description:

Cost of Living Dataset (2018).

The dataset was collected and developed by Numbeo a crowd-sourced global database of quality of life informations. This particular data is of the 2018 cost of living dataset which look at the Cost of Living Index, Rent Index, Groceries Index, Restaurant Price Index, and Local Purchasing Power Index of different countries which is further broken down to their respective cities. Other than the Indices, information on prices for daily/common commodities such as 1-bedroom apartment rent, 1-L milk, 1-kg of appples, etc. are also presented for each city and country. These indices are relative to New York City (NYC). Which means that for New York City, each index should be 100(%). If another city has, for example, rent index of 120, it means that on an average in that city rents are 20% more expensive than in New York City. If a city has rent index of 70, that means on average rent in that city is 30% less expensive than in New York City.

Design considerations

A strip plot is a graphical data anlysis technique for summarizing a univariate data set. It is a better alternative to a histogram or a density plot. It is typically used for small data sets (histograms and density plots are typically preferred for larger data sets) (NIST, 2015). A strip chart is handy when taking a look at a small set of one-dimensional data. It shows all the data, and since there are not too many points cluttering the plot, it is informative. It can also reveal gaps and outliers. The strip plot is most useful for showing subsets corresponding to a categorical variable (University of Iowa, 2020).

For this design, I grouped cities (data points) by their countries. I only filtered countries with a Local Purchasing Power Index Score of equal or greater than 100. This means that these countries will either be at par or higher with the standard Purchasing Power Index score of NYC. I calculated the mean Rental Index and represented it with a red circle to be more visible againts single colour of the data points. I also arranged the countries in the strip from the highest Rental Index to the lowest Rental Index. Hovering over it will show the reader additional information such as city and the actual rental cost for a 1-bedroom apartment. Hovering over the red label, will show the 'encoding' which is the mean Rental Index and the Country.

My realisation is that this strip plot would have best suited for entries with at least several data points. With the dataset I have, some countries only have one City as a data point. Sometimes, it is not so meaningful when compared to some of the other countries with several data points like USA, UK, or Canada, etc. Boxplots and histogram will also not be useful due to very few data points for most countries. However, this strip plot did allow me to look at variations, specially for countries with several data points and also allowed me to look at the average rental index and arranged them meaningfully. The visuals of course is a lot better than just mere histogram.
